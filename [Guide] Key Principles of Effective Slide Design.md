# Briefing Document: Key Principles of Effective Consulting Slide Design (McKinsey-Style)

This briefing document synthesizes key insights and practical takeaways from an analysis of 1,000 real McKinsey slides, leveraging a large language model (LLM) to identify common patterns and effective presentation strategies. The primary goal is to provide actionable guidance for creating clear, impactful, and consistent consulting presentations.

## Main Themes and Most Important Ideas/Facts:

### 1. Titles as Conclusions, Not Labels:
McKinsey slide titles are highly descriptive and convey the main conclusion or insight, rather than simply labeling the content.

- Key Fact: The average title length was 14 words, and "72% of the slides use active voice result-oriented titles."
- Impact: This approach allows for a "clear and consistent story from slide to slide," as each title contributes to a broader narrative, explaining "why that information actually matters."
- Example: A series of slides might flow from "companies have put innovation on the back burner during crisis" to "companies who invest in innovation during these times actually tend to outperform their peers," and finally, "they generate higher economic profit."

### 2. One Slide, One Job:
Each slide is designed to deliver a single, clear, and easy-to-understand message.

- Key Fact: "62% of slides delivered one clear easy to understand message." Even when incorporating "compound messages" (two points under a single theme), this number rose to "76%."
- Rationale: This principle emphasizes the message, preventing audience overload. McKinsey is "not afraid to use slides" to achieve this clarity.
- Example: Rather than combining "Tech companies greatly outperformed companies from other sectors" with "While their profile changed most top 10 tech companies in 2019 are digital attackers" on one slide, they are split into two distinct slides.

### 3. Simplicity and Clarity are Paramount:
Slides are designed to be concise and easily digestible, avoiding excessive text.

- Key Fact: The "average number of words on a slide was 100 words." Only "about 15% of the slides were considered dense," defined as "greater than 200 words," many of which were appendix slides.
- Technique: This simplicity is often achieved by "putting a big bold easy to understand chart right in the center of the slide."
- Principle: With an "unlimited number of slides," the focus is on "emphasize[ing] one thing at a time."

### 4. Repetition of Common Layouts:
McKinsey heavily relies on a small set of standardized slide layouts for consistency and efficiency.

- Key Fact: "Just four layouts accounted for about 70% of all the slides they used" across the entire dataset.
  - Most Common (29.7%): Single chart slide (descriptive title, chart in middle). This reinforces "simplicity: One chart one message clear descriptive title."
  - Second Most Common (18.7%): Single chart on the left with bullet points on the right.
  - Third Most Common (12.1%): Two-column comparison slide.
  - Fourth Most Common (10.4%): Table slide, effective for consolidating "a whole bunch of information into a single slide."

### 5. Strong Visual Emphasis:
Visuals, particularly charts, are central to communicating information.

- Key Fact: "71% of slides contained a chart of some kind," and this number jumps to "closer to 80%" when including visual frameworks.
- Rationale: "It's better to show someone than to tell someone," and "people tend to trust information when you've got hard numbers." Charts also help explain complex ideas that are "difficult to understand when you only use text."

### 6. Bar Charts Dominate:
Bar charts are overwhelmingly the most frequently used chart type.

- Key Fact: "Bar charts made up 40%" of all charts used, meaning "one in three slides had a bar chart in it." This includes both horizontal and vertical (column) charts.
- Other Common Charts: Line charts (15.9%) and waterfall charts (10.8%). Waterfall charts are particularly useful for showing "how to get from one value to another" and "the breakdown of a given value," simplifying complex data.

### 7. One Chart Per Slide (Often):
While visuals are key, the preference is often for a single, impactful chart per slide.

- Key Fact: "42.9% of all the slides had one chart," meaning "about 59% of slides that used a chart only used one chart."
- Principle: "Simplicity is king." While some slides may use two charts (approx. 15%), three or more are rare.

### 8. Strategic Use of Callouts:
Charts are enhanced with callouts to highlight specific insights or important data points.

- Key Fact: "About 72% of the charts had some kind of call out," which could be text-based or visual, to "help explain the information or to call attention to a specific part of the chart that was most important."

## Other Important Observations:
- Minimal and Intentional Color Use: McKinsey slides feature a "minimal use of colors," with color being used "very intentionally" to "call attention to a specific insight" or communicate messages through shading.
- Design Consistency: There is high "design consistency" within and across presentations, particularly in icons which "all follow the same design language." This creates a "distraction-free" experience due to a "really strong and consistent template."
- Top-Down Communication: Information flows logically from the descriptive title, breaking down into "subtitles or different sections." A "grid layout" often makes "the information easy to understand and easy to follow."

## Key Takeaways for Building Better Slides:
- Write Clear Titles: Titles should be conclusions, not just labels.
- Prioritize Simplicity: Don't overload the audience; aim for conciseness.
Strategic Visuals: Use charts effectively, typically one or two per slide for a clear message.
Ensure Consistency: Maintain a general level of consistency in design language and layout.
Focus on Insights: Always answer the question: "Why does this information matter for your audience?"
